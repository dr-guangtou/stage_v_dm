## Phase 1: SHMR Model Validation

### `shmr_validation.png`
**Created:** 2026-03-19 00:49 PDT

Stellar-to-halo mass ratio M*/Mh as a function of halo mass log10(Mh/Msun) at four redshifts (z = 0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0), using the Moster+2013 parameterization with fiducial parameters from their Table 1.

The x-axis spans log10(Mh) = 10 to 15 (in solar masses). The y-axis shows the dimensionless ratio M*/Mh from 0 to 0.07. Each curve represents a different redshift, color-coded: z=0.0 (blue), z=0.5 (orange), z=1.0 (green), z=2.0 (red).

At z=0, the SHMR peaks at M*/Mh = 0.038 (3.8% efficiency) at log Mh = 11.8, annotated with an arrow. This is the characteristic halo mass where galaxies convert baryons to stars most efficiently. Below the peak, the ratio falls as a power law with slope set by beta_0 = 1.376 (feedback from supernovae suppresses star formation in low-mass halos). Above the peak, it falls with slope gamma_0 = 0.608 (AGN feedback suppresses star formation in massive halos).

With increasing redshift: (1) the peak amplitude decreases monotonically (0.038 at z=0 to 0.019 at z=2), reflecting lower integrated star formation efficiency at earlier times; (2) the peak shifts to higher halo mass (log Mh = 11.8 at z=0 to 12.4 at z=2), because the characteristic mass M1 evolves as log M1(z) = 11.59 + 1.195 * z/(1+z); (3) the low-mass slope flattens at higher z (beta decreases from 1.38 to 0.83).

**Purpose:** Validates that the SHMR model implementation reproduces expected physical behavior before use in the halo model integrals. Acceptance test AT-1 (round-trip SHMR inversion to <0.01 dex) is verified separately in the terminal output.

## Phase 2: Halo Model Predictions

### `hod_occupation.png`
**Created:** 2026-03-19 01:12 PDT

Two-panel figure showing the halo occupation distribution (HOD) at z = 0.3 for four stellar mass bins: [9.5, 10.0], [10.0, 10.5], [10.5, 11.0], and [11.0, 11.5] in log10(Msun).

**Left panel — Central occupation N_cen(Mh):** Mean number of central galaxies per halo (0 to 1) as a function of log10(Mh). Each M* bin produces a bell-shaped curve centered at the halo mass where the mean SHMR hits the bin center. The width is set by the SHMR scatter sigma_logMs = 0.15 dex. Peak N_cen ~ 0.90 for all bins (less than 1.0 because a 0.5-dex bin captures ~90% of the Gaussian). The curves shift rightward with increasing M* (lower-mass galaxies reside in lower-mass halos). The [9.5, 10.0] bin peaks at log Mh ~ 11.4; the [11.0, 11.5] bin peaks at log Mh ~ 13.8.

**Right panel — Satellite occupation N_sat(Mh):** Mean number of satellite galaxies per halo (log scale, 10^{-5} to 100) vs log10(Mh). Uses a Leauthaud+2011 prescription with fixed parameters (alpha_sat = 1.0, M1_sat/Mmin = 17). Satellites appear only above a threshold halo mass (M_min, where N_cen_threshold = 0.5) and rise as a power law at high Mh. The onset shifts to higher Mh for higher M* bins. At the peak, satellite fractions are ~8% for [9.5, 10.0], ~9% for [10.0, 10.5], ~26% for [10.5, 11.0], and ~56% for [11.0, 11.5] — the highest M* bin is satellite-dominated because massive galaxies are often satellites in group/cluster halos.

**Purpose:** Validates that the HOD derived from the SHMR produces physically reasonable occupation functions before computing ΔΣ and clustering observables.

### `delta_sigma_model.png`
**Created:** 2026-03-19 01:12 PDT

Galaxy-galaxy lensing signal ΔΣ(R) as a function of projected physical radius R (Mpc) at z = 0.3, for four stellar mass bins and two single-NFW reference halos.

Both axes are logarithmic. The x-axis spans R = 0.1 to 30 physical Mpc. The y-axis shows ΔΣ in Msun/pc^2 from 0.01 to 500.

Solid colored lines show the halo-model bin-averaged ΔΣ for bins [9.5, 10.0] (blue), [10.0, 10.5] (orange), [10.5, 11.0] (green), [11.0, 11.5] (red). These are computed by integrating the NFW ΔΣ weighted by the HOD and halo mass function. The signal increases monotonically with M* bin because higher stellar mass corresponds to more massive halos with stronger lensing signals. All profiles decline approximately as R^{-1} at large radii, as expected for NFW.

Gray dashed and dotted lines show single-NFW halos at Mh = 10^{12} and 10^{13} Msun for reference. The [10.0, 10.5] bin sits below the 10^{12} reference (mean halo mass ~10^{11.8}). The [11.0, 11.5] bin exceeds the 10^{13} reference at small R due to contributions from very massive halos in the HOD integral tail.

**Purpose:** Validates the bin-averaged lensing signal against known single-halo profiles. These model predictions are the "signal" in the Fisher matrix; the covariance module provides the "noise."

### `phase2_summary.png`
**Created:** 2026-03-19 01:12 PDT

Four-panel summary diagnostic of halo model observables across stellar mass and redshift.

**Top-left — ΔΣ(R=1 Mpc) vs M* bin center at z = 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0:** Lensing signal amplitude at a fixed physical radius of 1 Mpc, plotted against the center of each stellar mass bin. Log scale y-axis (0.3 to 15 Msun/pc^2). Shows a steep positive correlation: higher M* → more massive halos → stronger signal. At fixed M*, higher z gives a stronger signal because the SHMR evolution maps the same M* to a higher mean Mh at higher z. The z=1 curve is ~3–5x above z=0.1.

**Top-right — Effective halo bias b_eff vs M* bin center:** Linear halo bias weighted by the HOD. Ranges from ~0.75 (low M*, low z) to ~2.1 (high M*, z=1). Bias increases with M* (more massive halos are rarer density peaks) and with z (at fixed mass, halos are more biased at higher z because they represent more extreme fluctuations in the earlier, less-evolved density field).

**Bottom-left — Galaxy number density n_gal vs M* bin center:** Comoving number density (Mpc^{-3}, log scale) from the halo model integral. Ranges from ~3×10^{-3} at [9.5, 10.0] to ~10^{-4} at [11.0, 11.5]. Decreases with M* (exponential cutoff of the stellar mass function) and with z (SHMR evolution reduces the abundance of galaxies at fixed M*). The z=0.1 and z=0.3 curves are close; the z=1.0 curve is suppressed by ~2–5x depending on the bin.

**Bottom-right — ΔΣ(R) profile comparison: bin-averaged vs single NFW for [10.5, 11.0] at z=0.3:** Blue solid line is the HOD bin-averaged signal; red dashed line is a single NFW at the mean halo mass (log Mh = 12.4). The bin-averaged signal is enhanced at small R (R < 0.5 Mpc) relative to the single NFW because the HOD integral includes a tail of more massive halos that boost the inner signal. At large R (R > 3 Mpc) the curves converge. This demonstrates the effect of HOD weighting beyond a simple mean-mass approximation.

**Purpose:** Comprehensive diagnostic confirming that all three halo model observables (ΔΣ, b_eff, n_gal) behave as expected across M* and z before entering the Fisher matrix computation.

## Phase 3: Covariance & Fisher Matrix

### `covariance_diagnostic.png`
**Created:** 2026-03-19 02:03 PDT

Four-panel diagnostic of the covariance model ingredients.

**Top-left — Effective Σ_crit vs lens redshift:** The effective critical surface mass density (Msun/pc^2), averaged over a Smail-type source n(z) with z_median = 1.0, as a function of lens redshift z_l from 0.05 to 1.5. Σ_crit_eff has a minimum (~2900 Msun/pc^2) near z_l ~ 0.35, where the lens-source geometry is optimal (D_ls/D_s is maximized). It rises at both low z (lens too close, small D_l makes the denominator small... actually Σ_crit ∝ D_s/(D_l D_ls) which diverges when D_l → 0) and high z (fewer source galaxies behind the lens, D_ls shrinks). The lensing signal-to-noise is best near the Σ_crit minimum.

**Top-right — Effective source density n_source_eff vs z_lens:** The fraction of source galaxies behind the lens multiplied by n_total = 25 arcmin^{-2}. At z_l = 0.1, nearly all sources are behind the lens (n_eff ~ 25). By z_l = 0.8, only ~17 arcmin^{-2} remain. By z_l = 1.4, only ~5. This drives the degradation of lensing constraints at high lens redshift.

**Bottom-left — σ(ΔΣ) vs R for three N_lens values at z_l = 0.3:** Shape noise error on ΔΣ (Msun/pc^2) at the optimal lens redshift, for N_lens = 10^4 (blue dashed), 10^5 (green solid), 10^6 (red dash-dot). Log-log axes. Errors scale as 1/√(N_lens) at all radii (factor of √10 between each curve). Errors decrease with R because annular area grows as R^2, providing more source-lens pairs. At R ~ 1 Mpc with N_lens = 10^5, σ(ΔΣ) ~ 0.05 Msun/pc^2.

**Bottom-right — Comoving survey volume vs z_max for three survey areas:** Volume (Mpc^3, log scale) from z_min = 0.05 to z_max, for areas of 1000, 5000, and 14000 deg^2. Scales linearly with area and as ~z_max^3 at low z, flattening at high z. A 14000 deg^2 survey to z = 1.0 covers ~2×10^{10} Mpc^3.

**Purpose:** Validates the noise model before Fisher matrix computation. Confirms AT-3 (variance scales as 1/N_lens) and checks that Σ_crit and survey volume are order-of-magnitude correct.

### `fisher_errors_comparison.png`
**Created:** 2026-03-19 02:06 PDT

Bar chart comparing marginalized 1σ fractional errors (σ/|θ_fid|, in percent) on the five z=0 SHMR parameters across three survey tiers: Stage-III (blue), Stage-IV (orange), Stage-V (green, using 9-parameter Fisher with evolution marginalized).

The x-axis shows the five parameters: log M1, N_0, β_0, γ_0, σ_{log M*}. The y-axis is logarithmic from ~10^{-3}% to ~1%.

Stage-V provides the tightest constraints on all parameters, followed by Stage-IV and Stage-III. The improvement from Stage-III to Stage-V is roughly 3–7x depending on the parameter. σ_{log M*} (the scatter) is the hardest parameter to constrain — its fractional error is ~0.8% for Stage-III, ~0.3% for Stage-IV, and ~0.11% for Stage-V. log M1 is the best-constrained parameter (<0.01% for all tiers).

**Important caveat:** These absolute error values are unrealistically tight (by ~100x) because the forecast includes only statistical noise (shape noise + cosmic variance), ignores systematic errors (photo-z, shear calibration, baryonic effects, model uncertainty), assumes diagonal covariance, and does not marginalize over nuisance parameters. The **ratios** between survey tiers are the meaningful scientific output, not the absolute values.

**Purpose:** Primary science result — demonstrates the relative constraining power of different survey generations on the galaxy-halo connection.

### `fisher_ellipses.png`
**Created:** 2026-03-19 03:15 PDT (updated with systematics)

Four panels showing marginalized 1σ Fisher forecast ellipses for selected parameter pairs, comparing Stage-III (blue), Stage-IV (orange), and Stage-V (green). All use 5-parameter Fisher (evolution fixed) for fair comparison. Each survey shows two ellipses: dashed = stat-only, solid = with systematics (5% floor + nuisance marginalization with σ_m=0.02, σ_Δz=0.03). All ellipses are centered on the fiducial Moster+2013 values.

**Top-left — log M1 vs N_0:** The characteristic halo mass vs the peak efficiency. Ellipses are nearly circular, indicating weak degeneracy. The solid (systematics) ellipses are visibly wider than dashed (stat-only), with the biggest inflation for Stage-III (which has fewest galaxies and is most affected by the systematic floor).

**Top-right — log M1 vs σ_{log M*}:** Characteristic mass vs scatter. The scatter axis shows the largest systematic inflation because σ_{log M*} is primarily constrained by lensing, which is most affected by the systematic floor. The solid Stage-III ellipse in σ_{log M*} is roughly 2–3x wider than the dashed version.

**Bottom-left — β_0 vs γ_0:** Low-mass slope vs high-mass slope. These show a positive degeneracy: increasing both slopes simultaneously produces similar observables. The systematic floor inflates the γ_0 direction more than β_0 (γ_0 is constrained by the high-mass lensing signal, which has larger absolute ΔΣ and thus larger systematic variance).

**Bottom-right — N_0 vs σ_{log M*}:** Peak efficiency vs scatter. Strong positive degeneracy visible in the tilted ellipses. Systematics inflate both axes but particularly the σ_{log M*} direction.

**Bottom-right — N_0 vs σ_{log M*}:** Peak efficiency vs scatter. Strong positive degeneracy — a higher normalization can be offset by larger scatter (more galaxies scattered out of a bin compensates for more scattered in). This is one of the most degenerate parameter pairs in the SHMR.

**Same caveat as fisher_errors_comparison.png:** absolute contour sizes are unrealistically small. Relative shrinkage between tiers is the meaningful comparison.

**Purpose:** Visualizes parameter degeneracies and how they are broken by more powerful surveys.

### `fisher_ngal_scaling.png`
**Created:** 2026-03-19 02:02 PDT

Constraint scaling with total galaxy count N_gal_total for a Stage-IV-like survey footprint (14000 deg^2, z = 0.05–0.4, log M*_min = 9.0), varying N_gal from 10^5 to 5×10^7.

Log-log axes. X-axis: N_gal_total. Y-axis: fractional marginalized error σ/|θ_fid| (percent). Five colored lines show the five z=0 SHMR parameters. A gray dashed line shows the reference 1/√N scaling.

σ_{log M*} (purple) shows the steepest improvement with N_gal, roughly tracking ~1/√N at high N. This is because the scatter is primarily constrained by lensing (which scales as 1/√N_lens). γ_0 (red) also improves noticeably. The other three parameters (log M1, N_0, β_0) show much flatter scaling — they are predominantly constrained by clustering (b_eff, n_gal), whose precision depends on the survey volume and the model-predicted tracer density (both independent of N_gal_total). The remaining improvement at high N_gal comes from the lensing contribution.

This demonstrates that: (1) adding more spectroscopic galaxies primarily improves lensing-dominated constraints; (2) for clustering-dominated parameters, the bottleneck is survey volume, not galaxy count; (3) the forecast correctly identifies which parameters benefit most from deeper spectroscopy.

**Purpose:** Validates that the Fisher machinery produces physically sensible scaling behavior and identifies which survey properties drive constraints on each parameter.

### `systematics_comparison.png`
**Created:** 2026-03-19 03:08 PDT

Two-panel comparison of Fisher forecast constraints with and without systematic errors.

**Left panel — Stage-IV, four error configurations:** Grouped bar chart showing marginalized fractional errors (sigma/|theta_fid|, percent, log scale) on the 5 z=0 SHMR parameters for: (1) stat-only (blue), (2) with 5% systematic floor (orange), (3) with nuisance parameter marginalization (green, sigma_m=0.02, sigma_dz=0.03), (4) both combined (red). The systematic floor has the largest impact, particularly on gamma_0 (~1.9x) and sigma_logMs (~2.8x), because these parameters are most sensitive to the lensing signal amplitude at radii where the floor dominates over shape noise. Nuisance marginalization adds a modest ~1-8% degradation.

**Right panel — All survey tiers, stat-only vs both systematics:** Side-by-side bars for Stage-III, IV, V comparing stat-only (lighter bars) to full systematics (solid bars). The relative ordering Stage-V < IV < III is preserved even with systematics. The absolute errors are more realistic with systematics enabled. The improvement from Stage-III to Stage-V is reduced from ~3-7x (stat-only) to ~2-5x (with systematics), because the systematic floor creates a noise floor that cannot be reduced by adding more galaxies.

**Purpose:** Validates that the systematic error implementation (1) inflates absolute errors as expected, (2) preserves the relative survey ordering, and (3) shows physically sensible parameter-dependent impact.
